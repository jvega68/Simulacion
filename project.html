    Dataset 2 - Sleep Requirements

    Background

    All known mammalian species spend at least part of each day sleeping, some species sleeping more than others. Sleep must serve some biological function, but why do sleep requirements vary so much from species to species? One approach to getting information that might help understand this question is to study the dependence of hours of sleep on species characteristics. The problem of interest is to understand the dependence of hours of sleep on the other species characteristics recorded in the data.

    Sleep: Total sleep, hrs/day

    Body: Body weight, log2(kg)

    Brain: Brain weight, log2(g)

    Life: Maximum life span, log2(years)

    Gest: Gestation time, log2(days)

    Danger: Danger index, 0 = relatively low danger from other animals, 1 = relatively high danger from other animals

    [Note that the units for some of the predictors are logarithmic (to base 2). This "transformation" is often made for biological data like these and often leads to better-fitting models. One way to think about this transformation is that when, for example, gestation time (in days) doubles, Gest (which is gestation time in log2(days)) increases by 1. In terms of analyzing the data, you don't need to worry about this - just take the data as they are.]

    Experiment and data

    We have data on 51 species, covering many different orders of mammals. This is surely not a random sample of species, so some care must be taken in generalizing any results to all mammal species. The variables defined above are species averages.     Dataset 3 - CPU faults

    Background

    Some computers divide memory into pages. When a program runs, it is allocated a certain number of pages of RAM. The program itself may require more pages than were allocated. When this is the case, currently unused pages are stored on disk. From time to time, a page stored on disk is needed; this is called a page fault. When a page fault occurs, one of the currently active pages must be moved to disk in order to make room for the page that must be brought in from disk. The trick is to choose a "good" page to send out to disk, where a "good" means a page that will not be used soon.

    Experiment and data

    The experiment consists of running different programs on a computer under different configurations and counting the number of page faults. There were two paging algorithms to study, and this is the factor of primary interest. A second factor with three levels was the sequence in which system routines were initialized. Factor three was the size of the program (small, medium or large memory requirements), and factor four was the amount of RAM memory allocated (large, medium or small). The data is given as follows:

    • Dataset 4 - Moral integration

    Background

    Angell's index of "moral integration" (i.e. social integration) combines information on the incidence of crime with data on social welfare expenditures. Cities that have low crime rates and high levels of welfare effort are assigned high moral-integration scores. The ethnic-heterogeneity index is calculated from the proportions of nonwhites and foreign-born whites residing in the cities, and the mobility index is constructed from the proportions of residents moving into and out of the cities. Angell argues that the moral integration of cities should decrease with ethnic heterogeneity and geographic mobility.
