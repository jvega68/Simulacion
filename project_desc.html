Dataset 1 - Catnip Bonanza

Background

The Berea, Ohio, animal shelter has a "no-kill" policy, which means that several cats live at the shelter for months. During shifts, healthy cats are allowed to wander the shelter, as cooping them up for the duration would be cruel. Inevitably, however, some of them tend to be less tolerant of sharing their domain with others and lash out, possibly hurting one another. An experiment was conducted to study whether ingesting catnip causes a decrease in amiability in resident cats of the shelter. It had been noticed that while catnip seemed to elicit silly, playful behavior, it also appeared to cause more fights between the animals, especially between notoriously crabby residents. While observing the cats after eating catnip is fun, if this herb did enhance frequency of negative interactions, perhaps shift workers should allow only the friendlier cats to consume it. The results of this study can hopefully help manage shifts to minimize hostility among residents.

Experiment and data

The experiment employed 15 randomly chosen feline residents. Each was placed in the middle of a room with 20 other residents (same for all trials), and negative interactions instigated by the subject were recorded for 15 minutes to establish baselines of amiability for each. Instances where the subject was the victim of another’s negative behavior were not recorded (negative behavior included swatting, hissing, biting, etc.). Residents were then given a teaspoon of "Cosmic Catnip," and negative interactions were measured for a second period of 15 minutes, beginning as soon as the subject finished eating. Dataset 2 - Sleep Requirements

Background

All known mammalian species spend at least part of each day sleeping, some species sleeping more than others. Sleep must serve some biological function, but why do sleep requirements vary so much from species to species? One approach to getting information that might help understand this question is to study the dependence of hours of sleep on species characteristics. The problem of interest is to understand the dependence of hours of sleep on the other species characteristics recorded in the data.

Sleep: Total sleep, hrs/day

Body: Body weight, log2(kg)

Brain: Brain weight, log2(g)

Life: Maximum life span, log2(years)

Gest: Gestation time, log2(days)

Danger: Danger index, 0 = relatively low danger from other animals, 1 = relatively high danger from other animals

[Note that the units for some of the predictors are logarithmic (to base 2). This "transformation" is often made for biological data like these and often leads to better-fitting models. One way to think about this transformation is that when, for example, gestation time (in days) doubles, Gest (which is gestation time in log2(days)) increases by 1. In terms of analyzing the data, you don't need to worry about this - just take the data as they are.]

Experiment and data

We have data on 51 species, covering many different orders of mammals. This is surely not a random sample of species, so some care must be taken in generalizing any results to all mammal species. The variables defined above are species averages. Questions

  1. Ignoring Body, Brain, and Life for now, how does the dependence of Sleep on Gest differ for different levels of Danger?

  2. Now considering all 5 predictors, what is a good multiple linear regression model that describes the dependence of Sleep on Body, Brain, Life, Gest, and Danger?

  3. What does your final model from Question 4 mean in terms of the association between Sleep and Body, Brain, Life, Gest, and Danger?

  4. Based on your final model from Question 4, how much should I expect the average amount of sleep for mammals with Body=2, Brain=5, Life=4, Gest=7, and Danger=0 to be? How much should I expect an actual mammal with Body=2, Brain=5, Life=4, Gest=7, and Danger=0 to sleep?

Hints for a good analysis

  1. Decide which of the four models (equal intercepts and slopes, different intercepts / same slopes, same intercepts / different slopes, same intercepts and slopes) is most appropriate.

  2. There are a variety of methods you can employ to come up with a good model – just write up what seems to you to be the most satisfactory analysis.

  3. Interpret the estimates in your model to answer this question.

  4. Any of the 5 predictors that don’t appear in your model can be ignored. Also, make sure you give some indication of the variability in your answer here and not just a "best guess".
